What is TrueHeat?

Verifiable provenance for aerospace raw material — so a Western buyer can trust a Chinese supplier's certificate in eight seconds, not hope it's real.
Confidential pilot pitch · 2026

TrueHeat lets a Chinese aerospace supplier prove the authenticity of their mill test reports to a Western buyer — by replacing (or augmenting) the easy-to-forge paper PDF with a cryptographically signed digital credential the buyer can verify in eight seconds with their phone.

The Problem

Counterfeit material is reaching primary aircraft structures

Every shipment of aerospace-grade raw material — a slab of Inconel, a titanium billet, a forged turbine disk — travels with paper certificates. A Mill Test Report (chemistry, mechanicals), a Certificate of Conformance, sometimes a chain of inspection records. These certificates are PDFs with corporate letterheads and JPG-of-a-stamp graphics. They are trivial to forge on any laptop.

Two recent cases tell the story.

2024 · AOG Technics
$53M · 180+ engines
A UK distributor sold ~60,000 CFM56 parts with forged FAA 8130-3 airworthiness tags. American, Delta, United, Southwest, and Ryanair were all affected. Founder jailed in 2026.
2024 · Spirit / Boeing / Airbus
787 · 737 MAX · A220
An unnamed Chinese supplier forged Certificates of Conformity falsely claiming Baoji Titanium as mill of origin. Counterfeit titanium reached primary aircraft structures. FAA investigation open.

After Spirit, every major Western OEM (Boeing, Airbus, RTX, Honeywell) ran a top-to-bottom risk review of their Chinese suppliers. Honest Chinese suppliers — who make most of the world's aerospace-grade raw material — now have a trust problem they can't solve with paper.

What We Built

Two surfaces: a supplier portal and a buyer verifier

A supplier portal. A Chinese QA manager logs in, fills in the same chemistry and mechanical data she fills in today (in Excel or paper). She clicks "Issue Credential." The portal generates a PDF that looks identical to her old paper cert — same letterhead, same red company stamp, same chemistry table — plus a small QR code in the corner.

A buyer verifier page. A Western receiving inspector scans the QR with his phone. In one second a webpage loads showing: ✓ Verified, the supplier's identity, the chemistry he's holding in his hand, and a tamper-check.

The supplier pays a subscription. The buyer pays nothing, ever. There is no app to install, no account to create, no integration to negotiate.

How It Works

One shipment, end to end

Follow a single batch of Inconel 718 from Longda Precision Materials in Wuxi to Spirit Aerosystems in Wichita.

  1. At Longda, Mrs. Liu's lab tech runs chemistry on a fresh heat (LD-2026-0847). Instead of typing the result into Excel and printing a paper cert, the tech enters the same numbers into the TrueHeat portal and clicks Issue.
  2. The portal signs the data with Longda's cryptographic key (stored in Longda's office, not ours). It generates a PDF that looks just like the old paper one — with a QR code added.
  3. Once a day, our service in Hong Kong batches every credential issued that day into a single cryptographic fingerprint and publishes it on the public Ethereum blockchain. This costs us $5–20 in network fees. Nothing identifiable goes on the chain.
  4. The shipment leaves Wuxi with the PDF emailed to Spirit and a printed copy stapled to the dock receipt — the same workflow Mrs. Liu has used for years.
  5. Six weeks later in Wichita, receiving inspector Greg pulls the box, sees the QR on the printed cert, and scans it. In one second his phone shows: ✓ Verified, the supplier identity, the exact chemistry he is holding, and the Ethereum block where it was anchored.
  6. If the cert had been forged — somebody edited the PDF, somebody photoshopped a fake QR onto a counterfeit, somebody made up a fake supplier — the page turns red and tells Greg exactly what's wrong. He rejects the material at the dock, not after it gets bolted to an airplane.

That's the entire product, from both sides.

Where the Data Lives

Four storage layers across three jurisdictions

A signed credential is not one thing in one place. It's four kinds of data, each living in the jurisdiction that's legally and practically right for it. A buyer scanning a QR is hitting some of these layers and never touching others — that's the whole compliance story.
China · L1
RAW SOURCE
Full mill data + original PDFs
Chemistry, mechanicals, customer POs, pricing, the lab tech's full report. Stored on Alibaba or Tencent Cloud in a Shanghai Free Trade Zone region. Encrypted at rest. Never leaves China by default.
→
Hong Kong · L2 + L3
QUERYABLE METADATA · L2
Signed credential payload
The structured JSON of the credential: alloy, heat ID, chemistry values, supplier DID, parent links, signature. AWS Hong Kong Postgres. Drives the verifier page.
REDACTED PDFS · L3
What the buyer actually sees
Same MTR as L1, but export-controlled fields blacked out. AWS Hong Kong or IPFS. Hash of both full and redacted versions on-chain so buyer can verify the redaction is faithful.
→
Public · L4
TAMPER-PROOF ANCHOR
Cryptographic fingerprints
One 32-byte Merkle root per day on Ethereum mainnet + per-credential revocation status on Polygon. Nothing identifiable on-chain — just bytes. Permissionless, neutral, outlives the company.

Plus, separately: the supplier's signing key lives in an HSM in the supplier's own office. We do not hold it. If we vanish tomorrow, every credential they've ever issued is still verifiable from public Ethereum — only new issuance stops.

Follow one number

Tracing "Ni = 53.1%" through the system

Wang Jing, a lab tech at Longda, runs chemistry on heat LD-2026-0847. The first number she types is Ni = 53.1%. Here is everywhere that number ends up.

Step What happens Where
1 Wang Jing types 53.1 into the TrueHeat portal in her browser. Browser sends it to our PRC-side server. China L1
2 Server saves the full structured credential — chemistry, mechanicals, PO, pricing — and the original PDF rendering. China L1
3 Server asks Longda's HSM (in their own server room) to sign the credential. The signature is just bytes — not data. China (HSM)
4 Signed credential payload — including Ni: 53.1 as a field — is transmitted to AWS Hong Kong. The PRC-side full PDF stays put. → HK L2
5 Hong Kong generates a redacted PDF (pricing and customer PO blacked out for export controls, chemistry retained). Stored on IPFS / S3. HK L3
6 At midnight HK time, our service batches every credential issued today into one Merkle root and publishes it to Ethereum mainnet. Cost: $5–20. HK → Ethereum L4
7 Greg at Spirit scans the QR. His phone hits the verifier page, which queries L2 (metadata + signed value 53.1), fetches the redacted PDF from L3, and verifies the anchor on L4. HK L2+L3 + L4
8 If Greg wants the full original (unredacted) PDF, he requests it from Longda directly. We do not push raw PRC data across the border by default. stays L1

So Ni = 53.1 lives in three places: as a raw value in PRC (L1), as a signed field in the credential payload in Hong Kong (L2), and as a tiny contribution to a daily hash on Ethereum (L4). Each copy serves a different purpose.

What crosses each border

The compliance map

Crosses the China border (CAC cross-border data flow)

Goes out
Signed credential payload (structured JSON: alloy, heat ID, chemistry numbers, mechanicals, mill of origin, lab tech ID, timestamp, signature). Per the 2024 CAC Provisions on Promoting Cross-Border Data Flows, mill chemistry is non-personal, non-"important" data — exempt from security assessment.
Stays in China
The original unredacted PDF with letterhead and stamp. Customer pricing. Customer PO. Internal lab notes. Anything the supplier doesn't want visible to non-customers. verify.trueheat.io never reads from L1.

Crosses to the public chain (Ethereum)

Goes on-chain
A 32-byte hash. That's it. One per day for the Merkle root of all credentials issued. Plus per-credential revocation flags on Polygon (also hashes only, no readable data). To anyone watching the chain, this looks like noise — useless without our L2 database.
Stays off-chain
Everything humans care about. The supplier's identity, the chemistry values, the PDFs, the heat ID, the parent links. The on-chain hash only proves the off-chain data was published — it doesn't store the data itself.

Crosses to the US / EU (export-controlled material)

Goes to buyer
The L3 redacted PDF and the L2 metadata via the verifier page. Inconel chemistry stays visible because it's already disclosed in the AMS spec the buyer purchased to. Pricing, customer PO, and internal process parameters are blacked out for EAR ECCN 1C002 hygiene.
Buyer never sees
Anything role-gated. The full raw PDF (unless the supplier explicitly grants it). Other shipments. Other customers. Buyer can verify their credential and nothing else.
Resilience

What survives when something breaks

The four-layer split isn't elegance for its own sake. It's resilience to specific failure modes that have killed prior projects.

If this happens…

…every PRC server is seized tomorrow.
The buyer chain of trust survives. All previously issued credentials remain verifiable from L2 + L4. Only new issuance from Longda stops until they rebuild.
…China bans all cross-border anchoring overnight.
The Hong Kong service keeps anchoring. Suppliers keep signing locally with HSM keys. Their signed credentials cross the border as data attachments — same legal posture as emailing a signed PDF today.
…TrueHeat the company shuts down.
Every credential ever issued remains verifiable forever. The signatures are valid, the anchors are on public Ethereum, the supplier keys are in supplier HSMs. The verifier page would need to be re-hosted by anyone — code is open source — but trust does not die with us.
…Ethereum has a 100-block reorg.
No data loss. Daily Merkle root is re-anchored on the next confirmed block. We finalize after 32 blocks (~6 minutes) for the daily roots; per-credential revocation on Polygon is more forgiving since it's not the trust root.
…a US OEM demands a permissioned chain for their internal use.
Same credentials, multi-anchored. The W3C VC is the trust root — it can be anchored to Ethereum, Hyperledger Fabric, and the OEM's private chain at the same time. They get their network, we keep ours.
Why Us

Four choices that separate us from every prior attempt

Most prior attempts (TradeLens, IBM Food Trust, B3i) died for the same reason: a single big company tried to host a "consortium" that suppliers were supposed to join. Nobody trusts a network controlled by their biggest customer or competitor.

  1. Suppliers pay, buyers verify free. Reverses the broken consortium model. The party that benefits from proving authenticity is the party that funds the system.
  2. Documentation fraud first, physical tagging later. Forged paperwork is the tractable Year-1 problem. Molecular taggants and RFID can come in Year 2 once the data backbone exists.
  3. Neutral hosting — explicitly NOT a Chinese state chain. Ant Chain, FISCO BCOS, JD Chain, VeChain are all PRC-government-adjacent and Western OEMs will not trust them. We use public Ethereum (whose neutrality is the point) and a Hong Kong corporate entity (English common law, neutral arbitration).
  4. We never lead with the word "blockchain." Enterprise aerospace buyers have moved past crypto-bro pitches. We talk about "verifiable credentials" — the W3C standard the EU and US DHS already use for digital identity. Same cryptography, sane marketing.
Boundaries

What we are not

  • Not a chain-of-custody logistics company. We don't touch the metal. We don't ship anything. We don't move atoms.
  • Not a testing lab. We co-fund independent labs to spot-check suppliers in our pilot, but we're not the ones running the chemistry tests.
  • Not a cryptocurrency. No token, no ICO, no "TrueHeat coin." Ethereum is just the neutral substrate we anchor signatures to — plumbing, not product.
  • Not an OEM tool. We're a neutral utility any OEM, prime, or Tier-1 can recognize. Explicitly not building Boeing's or Airbus's in-house system for them.
